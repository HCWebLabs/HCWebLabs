Did You Know?
Your Website Is Tax Deductible

That $3,000 website doesn't actually cost you $3,000. The IRS treats your business website as a legitimate expense — which means a chunk of that investment comes right back to you at tax time.

Design, development, hosting, your domain, ongoing maintenance — it's all deductible. Here's how it works, what counts, and how much you could actually save.

Let's Talk About Your Site See What's Deductible
The Short Answer: Almost Everything

What You Can Write Off

If an expense is "ordinary and necessary" for running your business online, the IRS generally considers it deductible. Here's what that includes for your website:

Website Design & Development

The cost of hiring a professional to build your site. Payments to third-party developers are generally deductible as ordinary business expenses in the year they're made.

Web Hosting

Monthly or annual hosting fees are deductible as a business operating expense — reported on Schedule C under advertising, utilities, or other expenses.

Domain Registration

Your annual domain renewal fees (typically $10–$20/year) are deductible. Initial registration can be expensed or treated as a startup cost.

Maintenance & Updates

Ongoing costs to keep your site running — content updates, security patches, bug fixes — are deductible in the year they're incurred.

SEO & Marketing

Search engine optimization, content creation, online advertising, and digital marketing costs are all deductible as advertising expenses on Line 8 of Schedule C.

Software & Tools

Stock images, SSL certificates, analytics tools, email services, and any business-use software (at least 50% business use) are deductible expenses.

The IRS hasn't issued formal guidance specific to websites, but established rules for business expenses and software costs apply. Multiple CPA firms confirm this treatment. Always consult your tax professional for your specific situation.

The Tax Details (In Plain English)

How the IRS Treats Website Costs

Not all website costs are deducted the same way. Here's a simple breakdown of how different expenses are typically handled:

Third-Party Development

Current Expense

Payments to a web developer (like me) are generally deductible as ordinary and necessary business expenses in the year they're paid. If your website primarily serves advertising purposes, internal development costs can also be fully deducted.

Hosting, Domain & Subscriptions

Current Expense

Recurring annual costs — hosting fees, domain renewals, SSL certificates, email services, analytics tools — are deductible operating expenses in the year they apply. Report them on Schedule C under the appropriate category.

Software & Hardware

Section 179 Eligible

Business computers, servers, and purchased software can often be fully deducted in the first year under Section 179 (up to $1.22 million for 2024). This means you don't have to spread the cost over multiple years.

Startup Website Costs

Up to $5,000 First Year

If you build your website before your business officially launches, up to $5,000 in startup expenses can be deducted in your first year of operation. Amounts over $50,000 begin to phase out, with the remainder amortized over 60 months.

Sole proprietors report these on Schedule C (Form 1040). LLCs, partnerships, and S-corps have different forms but the same underlying deduction principles apply.
