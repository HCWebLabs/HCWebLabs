The People Behind the Numbers

Who You're Missing

Accessibility isn't only about screen readers and wheelchairs. It's about real people in your community who experience the web differently every single day.

Aging Eyes

Your customer squints at tiny gray text on a white background. They can't read your menu or find your phone number — so they call your competitor instead.

Shaky Hands

Arthritis, tremors, or a temporary injury make tiny buttons impossible to tap. If your site needs perfect aim to navigate, you're turning people away.

Can't Hear the Video

Your promotional video plays without captions. A deaf customer, a parent in a quiet waiting room, or someone on a loud job site gets nothing from it.

Cognitive Overload

Flashing animations, cluttered layouts, and confusing navigation overwhelm people with ADHD, dyslexia, or anxiety — and honestly, they frustrate everyone.

These aren't edge cases. Disabilities include aging vision, arthritis, hearing loss, concussions, and temporary injuries — conditions that affect people across every age group and every community.

Common Problems

What Goes Wrong on Most Websites

These aren't obscure technical failures. They're everyday problems that drive real customers away from real businesses — and most site owners don't even know they exist.

Text Nobody Can Read

Light gray text on a white background looks sleek in a mockup and invisible in sunlight. Low contrast is the #1 accessibility failure on the web, found on 79% of websites tested.

Images Without Descriptions

A screen reader hits your product photo and announces "image" — nothing else. That customer has no idea what you're selling. Over half of all websites have this problem.

Can't Navigate Without a Mouse

Many people navigate using only a keyboard — by choice or necessity. If pressing Tab doesn't move logically through your page, your site is a dead end for them.

Buttons Too Small to Tap

Tiny tap targets aren't just an accessibility issue — they frustrate everyone on a phone. If your "Contact Us" button requires surgical precision, people won't bother.

The Department of Justice finalized a rule in April 2024 requiring all state and local government websites to meet accessibility standards — with deadlines starting April 2026 for larger entities and April 2027 for smaller ones. Anderson County and its municipalities fall within those timelines.

How I Build

Building It Right from the Start

Accessibility isn't a feature I bolt on at the end. It's baked into every line of code from day one — which means it costs less, works better, and lasts longer than trying to fix problems after launch.

Clean, Semantic Code

Proper HTML structure means screen readers, search engines, and browsers all understand your content. Headings, landmarks, and form labels — done right the first time.

Readable by Design

Every color combination meets contrast standards. Text scales cleanly when people zoom in. Your content is legible on a sunny porch or a dim room — no squinting required.

Works Without a Mouse

Every button, link, and form field is reachable by keyboard with visible focus indicators. Tab through your site and everything just works.

Touch-Friendly on Every Device

Buttons big enough to tap, spacing that forgives shaky hands, and layouts that adapt from a 4-inch phone to a 27-inch monitor without breaking.

No Overlay Widgets

I don't use shortcut plugins or "accessibility toolbar" overlays. They don't work, they've been fined by the FTC, and they make your site a bigger legal target. I write real code.

Better Search Rankings

Accessible code is clean code — and Google rewards it. Proper headings, descriptive alt text, fast load times, and semantic structure all boost your visibility in search results.
