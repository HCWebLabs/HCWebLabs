The Fun Side of the Web

Fun Facts About the Internet,
the Web, and Websites

The internet was built by scientists, shaped by weirdness, and runs on undersea cables that sharks occasionally try to eat. Here are some of the best stories behind the technology we use every day.

From the literal bug that gave us "debugging" to a coffee pot that inspired the webcam — the history of the web is a lot more human (and a lot more fun) than most people realize.

Where It All Started

Origin Stories

Every piece of technology you use today has a surprisingly human story behind it.

1947

The First Actual "Bug"

When Grace Hopper's team found a moth stuck in a relay of the Harvard Mark II computer, they taped it into the logbook and wrote "First actual case of bug being found." The terms "bug" and "debugging" had been used loosely before, but this is the moment that made them stick — because it was literally a bug.

1989

The Web Was Called "Vague but Exciting"

When Tim Berners-Lee submitted his proposal for what would become the World Wide Web to his boss at CERN, Mike Sendall scribbled "Vague but exciting" on the cover. It wasn't even an official project. Berners-Lee was just given time to tinker — and he built the entire foundation of the modern web.

1991

The First Website Is Still Online

Tim Berners-Lee published the world's first website on August 6, 1991 at info.cern.ch. It was a plain text page explaining what the World Wide Web was and how to use it. A restored version is still live and you can visit it today — over 30 years later. By the end of 1993, there were only 623 websites in the entire world.

1993

The Web Was Made Free on Purpose

CERN could have patented the World Wide Web. Tim Berners-Lee refused. On April 30, 1993, CERN released the web's source code into the public domain — royalty-free, forever. Berners-Lee later said, "Had the technology been proprietary, it would probably not have taken off." He gave the web to the world. On purpose.

Weird and Wonderful

The Internet Is Stranger Than You Think

Behind the technology we take for granted are stories that sound made up — but aren't.

 The Webcam Was Invented for Coffee

The first webcam in the world was created at the University of Cambridge in 1991 — not for video calls or security, but so researchers could check whether the coffee pot in the break room was full without walking down the hall. It streamed a live image of the coffee pot to their desktops. The Trojan Room coffee pot became internet-famous and the camera ran for over a decade.

 The @ Symbol Was Chosen Because Nobody Used It

In 1971, Ray Tomlinson invented email and needed a symbol to separate the username from the host. He picked @ because it was the least-used symbol on the keyboard — nobody was using it for anything. Now it's one of the most recognized characters on earth, and every email address in existence uses it.

 The First Photo on the Web Was a Comedy Band

The first photograph ever uploaded to the World Wide Web, in 1992, was a promotional shot of Les Horribles Cernettes — an all-female parody pop group made up of CERN employees. It was edited in an early version of Photoshop, saved as a .gif, and the rest is history. The image that launched a billion cat photos was, fittingly, a joke.

 Sharks Attack the Internet

The internet runs on massive undersea fiber-optic cables that span the ocean floor. Sharks have been documented biting these cables — possibly because the electromagnetic fields attract them. Google even had to wrap their Pacific cables in Kevlar-like material to protect them. The internet literally has to be shark-proofed.

 There's Wi-Fi on Mount Everest

Since 2010, high-speed internet has been available on the way up Mount Everest. Climbers can check their email, post updates, and video call from base camp at over 17,000 feet. The signal is stronger than what some people get in their living rooms.

Names, Numbers, and Nonsense

How Things Got Their Names

The words we use every day on the internet have some surprisingly odd origins.

1987

GIF: The Debate That Will Never Die

Steve Wilhite created the GIF format in 1987 at CompuServe — before the web even existed. The pronunciation debate has raged ever since. Wilhite insisted it's "jif" (like the peanut butter), saying: "The Oxford English Dictionary accepts both pronunciations. They are wrong. It is a soft 'G.' End of story." The internet respectfully continues to disagree.

1978

The First Spam Email Was an Ad for Computers

In 1978, Gary Thuerk sent an unsolicited mass email to 600 ARPANET users advertising DEC computers. The recipients were furious. The word "spam" wasn't applied to junk email until 1993, when a Usenet user jokingly coined the term — named after the Monty Python sketch, not the canned meat.

1997

"Wi-Fi" Doesn't Stand for Anything

Most people assume Wi-Fi stands for "Wireless Fidelity." It doesn't. The name was invented by a branding company called Interbrand (the same firm that named the Prozac). The Wi-Fi Alliance hired them because "IEEE 802.11b" wasn't exactly rolling off the tongue. "Wi-Fi" was just catchy — and it stuck.

2004

Facebook Is Blue Because Zuckerberg Is Color-Blind

Mark Zuckerberg is red-green color-blind. Blue is the richest color he can see, so he chose it for Facebook's interface. "Blue is the richest color for me," he told The New Yorker. "I can see all of blue." Sometimes the biggest design decisions come from the simplest human reasons.

1994

Amazon Was Almost Called "Cadabra"

Jeff Bezos originally named his online bookstore "Cadabra" — short for abracadabra. He changed it when his lawyer misheard it as "cadaver" over the phone. The name Amazon was chosen because it started with "A" (for alphabetical listing advantage) and because it represented the biggest river — fitting for what Bezos hoped would be the biggest store.

Pre-1995

Domain Names Used to Be Free

Before 1995, registering a domain name didn't cost a dime. No .com fees, no renewal charges — just claim it and it's yours. Then a $100 fee for two-year registration was introduced, and the domain name industry was born. Today, premium domains sell for millions. The most expensive domain ever sold — Cars.com — went for $872 million.

By the Numbers

Numbers That Are Hard to Believe

The scale of the internet is genuinely hard to wrap your head around.

Over 1.9 Billion Websites Exist

But fewer than 200 million are actively maintained. The rest are parked, abandoned, or forgotten — digital ghost towns scattered across the internet. About 252,000 new websites are created every single day.

Google Handles 8.5 Billion Searches a Day

That's roughly 99,000 searches every single second. If the internet went down for one day, 3 billion Google searches and 196 billion emails would have to wait. Google processes more requests in a single day than the entire internet had users in 1995.

Over 70% of All Emails Are Spam

Despite decades of increasingly sophisticated filters, more than 70% of all email traffic is still spam. That first unsolicited mass email in 1978? It worked — Thuerk actually sold a few computers. And the internet has been paying the price ever since.

The Average Attention Span Is 8 Seconds

The average human attention span for a web page has dropped to about 8 seconds — one second less than a goldfish. That's why your website has to load fast, communicate clearly, and make a first impression in 0.05 seconds. Because it really does.

NASA's internet connection runs at 91 gigabits per second. The average U.S. household? About 0.2 gigabits. NASA could download an entire HD movie in about one millisecond.

Internet Firsts

The First Time Everything Happened

Every feature of the modern internet had to happen for the first time somewhere.

 First Online Pizza Order — 1994

The first pizza ever ordered over the internet was from Pizza Hut in 1994. It was a pepperoni pizza with mushrooms and extra cheese. Online ordering was considered experimental at the time. Thirty years later, most major pizza chains make the majority of their sales online.

 First Thing Sold Online — 1994

The first secure online retail transaction was a Sting CD — "Ten Summoner's Tales" — sold on August 11, 1994, through a website called NetMarket. The transaction was encrypted using PGP encryption, making it the first safe e-commerce purchase. The buyer paid $12.48 plus shipping.

 First YouTube Video — 2005

The first video ever uploaded to YouTube was called "Me at the zoo" — an 18-second clip of co-founder Jawed Karim standing in front of the elephant exhibit at the San Diego Zoo. It was uploaded on April 23, 2005. The video has since been viewed over 330 million times. It's still up.

 First Emoticon — 1982

Computer scientist Scott Fahlman proposed using :-) and :-( on a Carnegie Mellon bulletin board in 1982 to distinguish jokes from serious posts. He wrote: "I propose the following character sequence for joke markers: :-) Read it sideways." Forty years later, emojis are a multi-billion dollar cultural phenomenon — and it all started with a colon and a parenthesis.

 First .com Domain — 1985

The first .com domain name ever registered was Symbolics.com, on March 15, 1985, by a computer manufacturer called Symbolics, Inc. The company is long gone, but the domain has been active continuously for nearly 40 years — making it the oldest living .com on the internet.

Things You Use Every Day

The Tech Behind the Ordinary

The tools you use without thinking about have some great backstories.

CAPTCHA Makes You Do Free Work

Every time you click "I'm not a robot" or identify traffic lights, you're training Google's AI. reCAPTCHA was originally designed to digitize books — your responses helped translate scanned text that computers couldn't read. Now those "select all the crosswalks" tests are training self-driving car algorithms. You're working for free.

Cookies Were Named After Fortune Cookies

Web "cookies" — the small data files websites store on your computer — were named after the programming concept of a "magic cookie," which itself was inspired by the idea of a fortune cookie: a message hidden inside something you receive. Lou Montulli invented the HTTP cookie in 1994 while working at Netscape.

Most Internet Traffic Isn't Human

Bots — automated programs that crawl, scrape, index, and sometimes attack websites — make up a significant portion of all internet traffic. Google's crawlers, SEO bots, malware scanners, and spam bots are constantly active. The internet is busier than it looks, and most of the visitors aren't people.

The Internet Has a Patron Saint

St. Isidore of Seville, a 7th-century bishop, is considered the patron saint of the internet. He was proposed for the role in 1997 because he tried to record everything known in his time in a 20-volume encyclopedia called Etymologiae — essentially an early attempt at organizing all human knowledge. Sound familiar?
